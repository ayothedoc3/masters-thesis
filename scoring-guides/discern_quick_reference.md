# DISCERN Quick-Reference Scoring Card

## Physical Activity Content Evaluation -- Master's Thesis

**Purpose:** Rapidly and consistently score 200 items of online physical activity (PA) content across websites, YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram using the DISCERN instrument (16 questions) plus JAMA benchmarks (4 criteria).

**Search terms evaluated:**
1. "how to start exercising"
2. "best exercises to lose weight"
3. "strength training for beginners"
4. "physical activity guidelines"
5. "home workout routine"

---

## Scoring Scale

| Score | Label | Meaning |
|:-----:|:------|:--------|
| **1** | Definitely No | The quality criterion is not fulfilled at all |
| **2** | Mostly No | The quality criterion is fulfilled to a minimal extent |
| **3** | Partially | The quality criterion is fulfilled to some extent |
| **4** | Mostly Yes | The quality criterion is fulfilled to a considerable extent |
| **5** | Definitely Yes | The quality criterion is completely fulfilled |

> **Quick decision rule:** When in doubt between two adjacent scores, go with your first instinct. Do not deliberate. Consistency across 200 items matters more than perfection on any single item.

# SECTION 1: RELIABILITY OF THE PUBLICATION (Q1--Q8)

---

## Q1: Are the aims clear?

*Does the publication tell you what it is about? Is it clear what topics are covered and who the intended audience is?*

### What to look for
- An explicit statement of purpose, topic, or audience (e.g., "This guide is for beginners who want to start a workout routine")
- A title or introduction that accurately previews the content
- Clarity about what the content will and will NOT cover

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Title, subtitle, introductory paragraph, meta-description | Often has a clear headline + intro paragraph stating aims |
| **YouTube** | Video title, first 30 seconds of speech, description box | Title may be clickbait -- listen to the opening statement |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, caption, first 3 seconds of audio | Very compressed; aims may only appear as on-screen text overlay |
| **Instagram** | Post caption, first slide of carousel, reel opening text | Carousels often state aim on slide 1; reels may rely on caption |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Explicitly states the purpose AND the target audience. Example: "This video teaches absolute beginners how to start a strength training program safely." |
| **4** | Purpose is clear from context but not explicitly stated; audience is implied. Example: Title says "Beginner Strength Training Full Guide" -- clearly aimed at beginners, clearly about strength training. |
| **3** | General topic is apparent but scope or audience is vague. Example: "My Workout Routine" -- you know it is about exercise but not who it is for or what it covers specifically. |
| **2** | Aims are unclear or misleading; title/intro is vague or clickbait. Example: "This ONE Thing Changed My Life" -- could be about anything; exercise topic only emerges mid-content. |
| **1** | No discernible aims; impossible to tell what the content is about before consuming it. Example: A TikTok with no caption, no on-screen text, and the creator just starts demonstrating exercises without any framing. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** NHS webpage titled "Physical activity guidelines for adults aged 19 to 64" with a clear introductory paragraph stating what the page covers.
- **Score 4:** YouTube video titled "5 Best Exercises to Lose Weight at Home" -- purpose is clear from the title alone.
- **Score 3:** Instagram carousel titled "Workout Tips" -- general topic is clear but scope is undefined.
- **Score 2:** TikTok with caption "You NEED to try this" showing someone exercising.
- **Score 1:** Instagram reel of someone working out with only a music track, no text, no caption beyond emojis.

## Q2: Does it achieve its aims?

*Does the publication deliver on what it promised? Is the information relevant to its stated purpose?*

### What to look for
- Whether the content actually addresses the topic stated in the title/intro
- Whether it provides enough depth to meaningfully fulfill its promise
- Whether it stays on topic or drifts into tangential/promotional content

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Read/scan the full article body | Check whether all sections promised in the intro are actually covered |
| **YouTube** | Watch key sections (intro, middle, conclusion) | Videos may promise a full routine but only show 2 exercises before pivoting to selling a program |
| **TikTok** | Watch the full video (15--60 sec) | Extremely limited time; many will partially achieve aims at best |
| **Instagram** | Swipe through all carousel slides OR watch full reel + read caption | Caption may fulfill aims that the visual content does not |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Fully delivers on stated aims with adequate depth. All promised topics covered. |
| **4** | Mostly delivers; minor gaps or one promised topic given insufficient treatment. |
| **3** | Partially delivers; covers the topic but superficially, or addresses only half of what was promised. |
| **2** | Barely delivers; content is mostly tangential, promotional, or too shallow to be useful. |
| **1** | Does not achieve its aims at all; content is irrelevant to the stated purpose or entirely promotional. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article titled "Home Workout Routine for Beginners" provides a complete weekly schedule with sets, reps, rest periods, and progression guidance.
- **Score 4:** YouTube video titled "5 Best Exercises to Lose Weight" demonstrates all 5 exercises with form cues but rushes through the last two.
- **Score 3:** TikTok titled "Beginner Workout" shows 3 exercises in 30 seconds with no explanation of sets, reps, or frequency.
- **Score 2:** Instagram post titled "How to Start Exercising" but the content is mostly about the creator's personal journey with one vague tip at the end.
- **Score 1:** Video titled "Complete Guide to Strength Training" that is actually a 60-second ad for a paid training app.

## Q3: Is it relevant?

*Is it relevant to you as a person seeking physical activity information? Does it address the needs of someone looking for PA guidance?*

### What to look for
- Whether the information is applicable to a general audience seeking PA guidance (not niche athletes, not extreme diets)
- Whether it addresses common questions a beginner or general public member would have
- Whether the content is actionable and practical

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Overall scope and reading level | Health authority sites (NHS, WHO, CDC) are almost always highly relevant |
| **YouTube** | Content framing and target audience | Fitness influencer content may be relevant if generalizable |
| **TikTok** | Whether the advice is actionable in the short format | Brief tips can be relevant if practically useful |
| **Instagram** | Whether the post provides usable PA information | Motivational posts without actionable content score lower |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Directly addresses PA information needs; actionable, practical, appropriate for the search term audience. |
| **4** | Mostly relevant; practical information present but includes some tangential content. |
| **3** | Somewhat relevant; addresses the topic but may be too niche, too advanced, or mixed with unrelated content. |
| **2** | Marginally relevant; touches on PA but content is primarily about something else (e.g., a product, a personal story). |
| **1** | Not relevant; content does not meaningfully address PA information needs despite appearing in search results. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** WHO physical activity guidelines page -- directly relevant to anyone seeking PA information.
- **Score 4:** YouTube video on "strength training for beginners" that is good but includes a 3-minute supplement tangent.
- **Score 3:** Instagram post about "my fitness journey" that includes some generic workout tips.
- **Score 2:** TikTok that is primarily a before/after transformation with one line about "just go to the gym."
- **Score 1:** Content that appeared for "best exercises to lose weight" but is actually about a fad diet with no exercise component.

## Q4: Is it clear what sources of information were used?

*Is it clear what sources of information were used to compile the publication, other than the author or producer?*

### What to look for
- Explicit citations, references, or bibliography
- Mention of specific studies, guidelines, or expert sources (e.g., "According to the ACSM guidelines...")
- Links to source material or further reading
- Credentials of cited experts

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Reference list, in-text citations, hyperlinks, "Sources" section at bottom | Most formal format; references are expected in health content |
| **YouTube** | Description box for links/references, on-screen citations, verbal mentions of studies | "Studies show..." without naming the study = partial credit at best |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, caption, verbal mention | Rarely includes sources; expect very low scores |
| **Instagram** | Caption, tagged accounts, swipe-up links, final carousel slide | Some creators add references on the last carousel slide |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Specific sources cited with enough detail to find them (author names, publication names, links, DOIs). |
| **4** | Sources mentioned by name but not fully referenced (e.g., "The ACSM recommends..." or "A 2023 study in JAMA found..."). |
| **3** | Vague attribution (e.g., "Research shows..." or "Studies suggest...") without naming specific sources. |
| **2** | Author claims personal experience or credentials as the sole basis; no external sources mentioned. |
| **1** | No sources mentioned or implied; information appears to come from nowhere. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Website article with numbered in-text citations linking to PubMed, WHO guidelines, and ACSM position stands.
- **Score 4:** YouTube video where the creator says "According to a meta-analysis by Schoenfeld et al..." and shows the paper on screen.
- **Score 3:** Instagram carousel that says "Research shows that strength training 2-3x per week is optimal" without citing which research.
- **Score 2:** TikTok by a personal trainer who says "In my 10 years of experience..." with no external references.
- **Score 1:** TikTok showing a workout with music overlay, no spoken or written attribution of any kind.

---

## Q5: Is it clear when the information was produced?

*Is it clear when the information used or reported in the publication was produced? Can you tell when the content was created or last updated?*

### What to look for
- Publication date, upload date, or "last updated" date
- References to time-sensitive information that help date the content
- Whether the date is easy to find (not buried)

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Byline date, "Last reviewed" or "Last updated" tag, footer | Some sites have publication date but no update date; score 4 |
| **YouTube** | Upload date shown below the video title | Almost always visible; typically Score 5 |
| **TikTok** | Upload date visible on the post | Almost always visible; typically Score 5 |
| **Instagram** | Post date visible on the post | Almost always visible; typically Score 5 |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Clear date of publication/upload AND last-reviewed/updated date (if applicable). |
| **4** | Clear date of publication/upload but no indication of review or update status. |
| **3** | Date is present but hard to find, or only a vague time indicator (e.g., "recently"). |
| **2** | No explicit date but contextual clues allow approximate dating. |
| **1** | No date information whatsoever; impossible to determine when content was produced. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** NHS webpage with "Last reviewed: 14 October 2024" prominently displayed. YouTube video with visible upload date + creator says "Updated for 2025."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video with upload date visible (standard for all YouTube videos). TikTok with visible post date. Instagram post with visible date.
- **Score 3:** Website article where the date is buried in the page source or URL but not visibly displayed.
- **Score 2:** Website article with no date, but it references "the new 2020 guidelines" suggesting approximate age.
- **Score 1:** Website with no date, no contextual time clues, and no "last updated" information.

> **NOTE:** Social media platforms (YouTube, TikTok, Instagram) almost always display dates. Most will score 4--5 by default on Q5. Websites are more variable.

## Q6: Is it balanced and unbiased?

*Is the publication balanced and unbiased? Does it present information fairly without promoting a single approach?*

### What to look for
- Whether multiple approaches to PA are acknowledged (not just one "best" method)
- Whether the content avoids sensationalist or absolutist language ("the ONLY exercise you need," "NEVER do this")
- Whether commercial interests are disclosed or evident (selling a product, program, or supplement)
- Whether the content acknowledges that different approaches suit different people

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Check for disclosure statements, advertising, affiliate links, whether multiple approaches are discussed | Health authority sites tend to be balanced; commercial fitness sites often are not |
| **YouTube** | Listen for absolutist claims, check for sponsorship disclosures, note if the creator is selling something | Sponsorship =/= automatic low score IF disclosed and content is still balanced |
| **TikTok** | Note sensationalist framing ("STOP doing this!"), product promotion, one-sided claims | Format incentivizes extreme claims; expect low balance scores |
| **Instagram** | Check for product tags, affiliate links, absolutist claims in caption/slides | Influencer content frequently promotes specific products or programs |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Presents multiple approaches fairly, acknowledges individual differences, no commercial bias, balanced language. |
| **4** | Mostly balanced; may slightly favor one approach but acknowledges alternatives. Commercial links present but disclosed and do not dominate. |
| **3** | Somewhat biased; favors one method but does not actively disparage alternatives. May have undisclosed commercial interest. |
| **2** | Clearly biased; promotes one approach as the only/best option, uses sensationalist language, or is heavily commercial. |
| **1** | Entirely one-sided; absolutist claims, fearmongering, undisclosed commercial agenda, or actively demonizes legitimate alternatives. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** WHO guidelines page presenting aerobic, strength, flexibility, and balance activities as all beneficial, with individualized recommendations.
- **Score 4:** YouTube video recommending compound lifts for beginners but briefly mentioning that machines and bodyweight exercises are also valid starting points.
- **Score 3:** Article about "best exercises to lose weight" that only covers HIIT but does not claim other methods are useless.
- **Score 2:** Instagram post: "Cardio is KILLING your gains! STOP doing it!" -- promotes one view, disparages another.
- **Score 1:** TikTok selling a specific program: "Every other workout is a waste of time, link in bio to the only program that works."

## Q7: Does it provide additional sources of support and information?

*Does it provide details of additional sources of support and information? Does it tell you where to get more help?*

### What to look for
- Links or references to further reading, other resources, or professional guidance
- Suggestions to consult a doctor, physiotherapist, or certified trainer
- Links to helplines, organizations, or community resources
- Recommendations for follow-up actions

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | "Further reading" sections, sidebar links, embedded links to organizations | Most likely to have additional resources |
| **YouTube** | Description box links, verbal suggestions, pinned comments | "Check out my other video" counts minimally unless it points to genuinely additional information |
| **TikTok** | Caption links, "check bio," verbal suggestions | Very rare; most TikToks do not provide additional resources |
| **Instagram** | Caption links, "link in bio," tagged accounts of health organizations | "Link in bio" to their own product page does NOT count |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Provides specific additional resources: links to guidelines, suggests consulting professionals, lists organizations or helplines. |
| **4** | Mentions where to find more information or suggests consulting a professional, but details are limited. |
| **3** | Brief mention of "talk to your doctor" or one additional resource with no specifics. |
| **2** | Only points to the creator's own additional content (other videos, their website, their program). |
| **1** | No additional sources of support or information mentioned at all. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article that links to ACSM, local gym finder tools, and says "Consult a certified personal trainer or your GP before beginning a new program."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video that says "Check the description for links to the studies I mentioned and the NHS exercise guidelines."
- **Score 3:** Instagram post that says "Always check with your doctor first!" but provides no links or specifics.
- **Score 2:** TikTok that says "Part 2 on my page!" or "Full program link in bio" (pointing to their own paid product).
- **Score 1:** Content that ends with no reference to any additional resources whatsoever.

## Q8: Does it refer to areas of uncertainty?

*Does it refer to areas of uncertainty? Does it acknowledge where evidence is lacking, where there is debate, or where individual results may vary?*

### What to look for
- Acknowledgment that evidence is evolving or that experts disagree
- Language like "more research is needed," "results may vary," "this depends on individual factors"
- Honesty about limitations of the recommendations
- Avoidance of false certainty

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Look for hedging language, "limitations" sections, nuanced discussion | Health authority sites often acknowledge uncertainty; commercial sites rarely do |
| **YouTube** | Listen for qualifiers ("this may not work for everyone," "the evidence is mixed") | Longer format allows for nuance; look for it in the middle or end |
| **TikTok** | Listen/look for any acknowledgment of uncertainty | Extremely rare in short-form; format incentivizes certainty |
| **Instagram** | Check caption for nuance, qualifiers | Carousel last slides sometimes add caveats |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Explicitly discusses areas of uncertainty or debate, acknowledges limitations, notes where individual variation matters. |
| **4** | Mentions uncertainty or caveats in passing (e.g., "This works for most people, but results vary"). |
| **3** | Implies uncertainty without explicitly stating it; uses some hedging language. |
| **2** | Presents information as mostly certain with minimal qualification; one brief caveat at most. |
| **1** | Presents everything as absolute fact with no acknowledgment of uncertainty, debate, or individual variation. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article discussing optimal rep ranges that says "Research is mixed on whether 3 or 5 sets is superior for beginners; individual response varies significantly, and the best approach depends on your recovery capacity and schedule."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video that says "These are general guidelines -- your ideal routine will depend on your fitness level, any injuries, and your goals."
- **Score 3:** Website that uses language like "generally recommended" without explicitly discussing uncertainty.
- **Score 2:** Instagram post stating "You MUST train 5 days a week for results" with a small caveat: "listen to your body."
- **Score 1:** TikTok declaring "This is the perfect workout, follow this exactly" with zero qualification.

# SECTION 2: QUALITY OF TREATMENT INFORMATION (Q9--Q15)

> **KEY ADAPTATION:** In all questions below, "treatment" = exercise programs, workout routines, physical activity recommendations, or fitness approaches. "Condition" = the health concern or fitness goal being addressed (e.g., weight loss, strength, cardiovascular health, general fitness).

---

## Q9: Does it describe how each treatment works?

*Does it describe how each exercise or PA approach works? Does it explain the mechanism or rationale behind the recommendations?*

### What to look for
- Explanation of WHY certain exercises are recommended (not just WHAT to do)
- Description of how the exercise affects the body (e.g., "compound movements recruit multiple muscle groups, increasing calorie expenditure")
- Explanation of the physiological principles (progressive overload, cardiovascular adaptation, etc.)

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Body text explanations accompanying each recommendation | Articles have space for mechanistic explanations |
| **YouTube** | Verbal explanations during or between exercise demonstrations | Good creators explain WHY, not just WHAT |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text or brief verbal explanation | Very limited time; most only show the exercise without explaining how it works |
| **Instagram** | Caption explanations, carousel text slides between exercise images | Carousels can alternate exercise demos with explanation slides |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Clearly explains how and why each exercise/approach works, including the underlying mechanism or principle. |
| **4** | Explains the rationale for most recommendations; some exercises explained mechanistically, others just described. |
| **3** | Some explanation of how exercises work, but mostly just tells you what to do without explaining why. |
| **2** | Minimal explanation; lists exercises with form cues only, no rationale. |
| **1** | No explanation of how any exercise or approach works; pure demonstration or list with no reasoning. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article explaining "Squats build lower-body strength by loading the quadriceps, glutes, and hamstrings through a full range of motion. Progressive overload -- gradually increasing weight -- forces continued muscular adaptation."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video where the creator explains "We're doing compound movements because they work more muscles at once" but does not elaborate on the physiology.
- **Score 3:** Instagram carousel showing 5 exercises with brief descriptions ("Squats -- great for legs!") but no mechanistic explanation.
- **Score 2:** TikTok demonstrating a workout with only form cues ("keep your back straight, push through your heels").
- **Score 1:** TikTok of someone performing a workout set to music with no explanation whatsoever.

## Q10: Does it describe the benefits of each treatment?

*Does it describe the benefits of each exercise or PA approach? Does it explain what positive outcomes you can expect?*

### What to look for
- Explicit mention of benefits (weight loss, strength gains, improved cardiovascular health, mental health benefits, etc.)
- Whether benefits are realistic and specific rather than vague or exaggerated
- Whether benefits are attributed to specific exercises/approaches or just to "exercise" generally

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Benefits sections, bullet point lists of outcomes | Often structured with clear benefit descriptions |
| **YouTube** | Verbal discussion of benefits, on-screen text | Often discussed in the introduction as motivation |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, caption | May make exaggerated benefit claims; note this for Q6 as well |
| **Instagram** | Caption, carousel slides | Benefit claims in captions can be detailed |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Clearly describes specific, realistic benefits of each exercise or approach discussed. |
| **4** | Describes benefits for most exercises/approaches; some specific, some general. |
| **3** | Mentions benefits in general terms (e.g., "great for fat loss") without specificity or only for some exercises. |
| **2** | Vague or exaggerated benefit claims (e.g., "this will transform your body") with no specifics. |
| **1** | No mention of benefits at all, or benefits are entirely unrealistic/misleading. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** "Walking 150 minutes per week is associated with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease, improved blood sugar regulation, and reduced symptoms of anxiety and depression."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video: "Strength training helps you build muscle, strengthens your bones, and boosts your metabolism" -- accurate but not deeply specific.
- **Score 3:** Instagram post: "This workout is amazing for toning!" -- vague benefit for one routine.
- **Score 2:** TikTok: "Do this for a flat stomach in 2 weeks!" -- exaggerated, unrealistic claim.
- **Score 1:** Content that shows exercises with no mention of any benefits.

## Q11: Does it describe the risks of each treatment?

*Does it describe the risks of each exercise or PA approach? Does it mention potential injuries, contraindications, or safety concerns?*

### What to look for
- Mention of potential injury risks (e.g., knee strain from improper squat form)
- Contraindications (e.g., "avoid this if you have lower back problems")
- Safety precautions and form warnings
- Advice to consult a healthcare provider before starting

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Safety sections, warning boxes, contraindication lists | Most likely to include structured risk information |
| **YouTube** | Verbal safety cues, form warnings, disclaimers | Good creators include safety cues during demonstrations |
| **TikTok** | On-screen warnings, verbal cautions | Rarely included; format discourages risk discussion |
| **Instagram** | Caption disclaimers, carousel safety slides | Sometimes included in carousels as a final "safety" slide |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Clearly describes risks, contraindications, and safety precautions for the exercises discussed. Mentions when to see a professional. |
| **4** | Mentions key risks and includes form/safety cues, but does not cover contraindications comprehensively. |
| **3** | Brief mention of safety (e.g., "use proper form") without specific risk details. |
| **2** | Minimal risk acknowledgment; one passing comment like "be careful" or a text disclaimer. |
| **1** | No mention of risks, injuries, contraindications, or safety precautions whatsoever. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article: "Deadlifts carry a risk of lower back injury if performed with a rounded spine. Those with herniated discs should avoid this exercise or use a trap bar variant. Start with light weight and consider working with a certified trainer."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video with form corrections: "Make sure your knees track over your toes -- improper form here can lead to knee pain. If you have knee issues, try the modified version."
- **Score 3:** Instagram carousel with a final slide: "Remember to warm up and use proper form!"
- **Score 2:** TikTok with a text disclaimer: "Not medical advice" in small font but no actual risk discussion in the content.
- **Score 1:** Content demonstrating advanced exercises (e.g., heavy barbell movements) with zero safety information.

---

## Q12: Does it describe what would happen if no treatment is used?

*Does it describe the consequences of physical inactivity? Does it explain what happens if you do NOT exercise?*

### What to look for
- Discussion of the health consequences of a sedentary lifestyle
- Mention of what happens if PA goals are not pursued (e.g., continued weight gain, declining cardiovascular fitness)
- Context about why PA matters by contrasting it with inactivity

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Introductory sections, "Why exercise?" sections | Health authority sites often frame PA against inactivity risks |
| **YouTube** | Introduction and motivation segments | May discuss inactivity risks as motivation early in the video |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, caption | Rarely discussed; format does not lend itself to this topic |
| **Instagram** | Caption, introductory carousel slides | Sometimes used as a motivational framing |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Explicitly describes the consequences of inactivity with specific health outcomes (increased disease risk, muscle loss, mental health decline, etc.). |
| **4** | Mentions consequences of inactivity but without specific detail (e.g., "Sedentary lifestyles are linked to many health problems"). |
| **3** | Implies the consequences of inactivity without stating them directly (e.g., "Exercise is essential for preventing chronic disease"). |
| **2** | Very brief or vague reference (e.g., "You need to move more" without explaining why). |
| **1** | No mention of what happens without exercise or physical activity. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** "Physical inactivity is the fourth leading risk factor for global mortality. Sedentary adults have a 20--30% increased risk of all-cause mortality compared to those meeting PA guidelines."
- **Score 4:** YouTube intro: "If you're not strength training, you're losing muscle mass every year after 30, and that affects everything from your metabolism to your bone density."
- **Score 3:** Article that says "Regular exercise is crucial for long-term health" without explaining what happens without it.
- **Score 2:** Instagram caption: "Stop being lazy and start moving!"
- **Score 1:** Content that dives straight into exercises with no context about why PA matters.

> **NOTE:** Most exercise content does NOT address this question well. Expect many scores of 1--2, especially on social media. This is a valid finding.

## Q13: Does it describe how treatment choices affect overall quality of life?

*Does it describe how the exercise or PA approach affects overall quality of life? Does it address well-being beyond just physical outcomes?*

### What to look for
- Discussion of broader quality-of-life impacts: mental health, energy, sleep, social benefits, daily functioning
- Whether the content goes beyond purely physical/aesthetic outcomes
- Acknowledgment that PA affects multiple dimensions of well-being

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Sections on benefits beyond fitness; holistic framing | Health authority sites often address quality of life broadly |
| **YouTube** | Discussion of mental health, energy, sleep, daily life improvements | Longer format allows for quality-of-life discussion |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, verbal mention | Occasionally mentioned as motivational framing |
| **Instagram** | Caption, motivational framing | Common in wellness-oriented accounts but often vague |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Explicitly discusses how PA affects multiple quality-of-life domains (mental health, sleep, energy, social life, functional capacity, etc.). |
| **4** | Mentions quality-of-life benefits beyond physical outcomes in some detail (e.g., discusses mental health and energy improvements). |
| **3** | Brief mention of broader benefits (e.g., "exercise is great for your mental health too") without elaboration. |
| **2** | Focuses entirely on physical/aesthetic outcomes but makes one passing reference to well-being. |
| **1** | No mention of quality of life; content is entirely focused on physical/aesthetic outcomes or pure exercise demonstration. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** "Regular physical activity improves sleep quality, reduces anxiety and depression symptoms, increases energy levels throughout the day, and enhances social well-being through group activities."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video that dedicates a segment to: "Beyond the physical benefits, I noticed my sleep improved dramatically and my stress levels dropped."
- **Score 3:** Instagram post mentioning "Exercise is good for your mind and body!" without specifics.
- **Score 2:** TikTok focused entirely on "get shredded abs" with one throwaway line about "feeling great."
- **Score 1:** Content that is purely about physical aesthetics or exercise demonstrations with no mention of broader well-being.

---

## Q14: Is it clear that there may be more than one possible treatment choice?

*Is it clear that there may be more than one possible exercise approach or PA option? Does the content acknowledge that different routines or activities may suit different people?*

### What to look for
- Whether multiple exercise options are presented (not just one "best" routine)
- Whether the content acknowledges that different people may benefit from different approaches
- Whether alternatives or modifications are offered
- Whether the content avoids a one-size-fits-all approach

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Whether multiple approaches are discussed; whether alternatives are offered | Good articles compare options |
| **YouTube** | Whether modifications or alternatives are shown; whether different options are discussed | Some creators show beginner/intermediate/advanced options |
| **TikTok** | Whether any alternatives are mentioned | Format strongly favors a single "best" approach |
| **Instagram** | Carousel may show options; caption may mention alternatives | Carousels can present "Option A vs Option B" |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Clearly presents multiple PA options, compares approaches, and explains that the best choice depends on individual circumstances. |
| **4** | Presents the main approach but mentions alternatives or modifications for different fitness levels. |
| **3** | Briefly acknowledges that other options exist but focuses on one approach. |
| **2** | Presents one approach as the best/only option but implicitly allows that others exist. |
| **1** | Presents a single approach as the definitive answer with no acknowledgment of alternatives. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article: "For weight loss, you can choose walking, running, cycling, swimming, or strength training. Each has pros and cons. The best choice depends on your preferences, physical condition, and access to equipment. Here is a comparison..."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video: "Today I'm showing you a barbell routine, but if you don't have equipment, you can do bodyweight alternatives -- I'll show modifications."
- **Score 3:** Instagram post showing one workout with the caption: "There are lots of ways to get fit, but this is my favorite."
- **Score 2:** TikTok: "THE best exercise for weight loss is..." (singular, definitive, but it is implied others exist).
- **Score 1:** TikTok: "Stop doing everything else, THIS is the only workout you need."

## Q15: Does it provide support for shared decision-making?

*Does it provide support for shared decision-making? Does it help you make your own informed choice about which exercise approach is right for you?*

### What to look for
- Whether the content empowers the reader/viewer to choose what suits them
- Whether it provides criteria for decision-making (e.g., "choose based on your schedule, goals, and preferences")
- Whether it encourages consultation with a professional
- Whether it provides enough information to make an informed choice (not just "do this")

### Platform-specific guidance

| Platform | Where to check | Notes |
|:---------|:---------------|:------|
| **Website** | Decision aids, comparison tables, "How to choose" sections, recommendation to consult a professional | Best format for shared decision-making support |
| **YouTube** | Whether the creator helps viewers choose rather than prescribe | "Here are the factors to consider" vs. "Just do this" |
| **TikTok** | Whether any decision-making support is offered | Extremely rare; format incentivizes prescriptive advice |
| **Instagram** | Whether the caption helps people choose; whether carousels provide comparison info | Carousels comparing options support decision-making |

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | Actively helps the reader choose the right approach for their circumstances; provides decision criteria, comparison, and encourages professional consultation. |
| **4** | Provides some decision-making support (e.g., "Choose based on your goals and schedule") and encourages seeking professional advice. |
| **3** | Briefly suggests that individual choice matters but does not provide concrete decision-making support. |
| **2** | Prescriptive approach ("Do this routine") with a brief caveat like "adjust as needed." |
| **1** | Entirely prescriptive; presents a single protocol as the answer with no decision-making support. |

### Common PA content examples

- **Score 5:** Article with a section: "How to choose the right exercise program for you: Consider your current fitness level (take this self-assessment), your time availability, equipment access, and any existing health conditions. Discuss your plan with your GP or a certified exercise professional."
- **Score 4:** YouTube video: "I've given you three options today. If you're a total beginner, start with option 1. If you have more experience, try option 3. And always check with your doctor if you have any health concerns."
- **Score 3:** Instagram carousel that says "Find what works for YOU" on the last slide but does not provide criteria for choosing.
- **Score 2:** TikTok that prescribes a specific routine with the caveat "modify if needed."
- **Score 1:** Content that says "Follow this program exactly for 12 weeks" with no acknowledgment of individual needs.

## Q16: Overall rating of the publication

*Based on your answers to all previous questions, rate the overall quality of the publication as a source of information about physical activity.*

### What to consider
- Your overall impression of the publication's trustworthiness, completeness, and usefulness
- This is a holistic judgment -- it should reflect your gestalt impression, not simply the mean of Q1--Q15
- Weight reliability (Q1--Q8) and treatment quality (Q9--Q15) roughly equally
- Consider: would you recommend this to a friend who asked about exercise?

### Quick scoring decision tree

| Score | Criteria |
|:-----:|:---------|
| **5** | **High quality with minimal shortcomings.** Reliable, evidence-based, balanced, comprehensive, and actionable. You would confidently recommend it. Typically: health authority websites, well-sourced long-form articles, or detailed evidence-based YouTube channels. |
| **4** | **Good quality with minor shortcomings.** Generally reliable and useful but has some gaps (e.g., missing risk information, limited references, slight bias). You would recommend it with minor reservations. |
| **3** | **Moderate quality with some important shortcomings.** Useful information is present but mixed with gaps, bias, or lack of evidence. You might recommend it with caveats ("take it with a grain of salt"). |
| **2** | **Low quality with several important shortcomings.** Unreliable, biased, superficial, or misleading. Limited useful information. You would not recommend it. Typical of most short-form social media PA content. |
| **1** | **Very low quality with critical shortcomings.** Potentially harmful, entirely unsourced, grossly misleading, or so superficial as to be useless. You would actively warn someone away from it. |

### Calibration benchmarks

| Score | Typical content |
|:-----:|:----------------|
| **5** | WHO/NHS/CDC physical activity guidelines; well-sourced academic or clinical PA websites |
| **4** | Reputable health journalism (e.g., well-sourced Healthline or Mayo Clinic articles); evidence-based fitness YouTubers with citations |
| **3** | Decent fitness blog posts; YouTube videos with good advice but no sources; comprehensive Instagram carousels |
| **2** | Typical fitness influencer content with exaggerated claims; generic workout lists without context |
| **1** | Misinformation, dangerous advice, content that is entirely promotional with no informational value |

## Websites

## YouTube

| Aspect | Guidance |
|:-------|:---------|
| **Where to check for credentials** | Channel "About" page, video description, verbal self-introduction, on-screen text |
| **Where to check for dates** | Upload date visible below the title (almost always present) |
| **Where to check for references** | Description box (scroll down), on-screen citations, pinned comments |
| **Where to check for disclosure** | "Includes paid promotion" label, verbal sponsorship disclosures, description box disclosures |
| **Typical scoring pattern** | Moderate; longer format allows for depth. Reliable channels may score 3--5 on most questions. Low-quality channels may score 1--3. |

> **Shortcut for YouTube:** Jump to 0:00--1:00 for aims (Q1), skip to middle for content quality (Q9--Q15), jump to end for additional resources (Q7) and uncertainty (Q8). Check description box for sources (Q4) and disclosure.

## TikTok

| Aspect | Guidance |
|:-------|:---------|
| **Where to check for credentials** | Creator bio, profile page |
| **Where to check for dates** | Post date on the video (almost always visible) |
| **Where to check for references** | On-screen text, caption, comments (rare) |
| **Where to check for disclosure** | "Paid partnership" labels, verbal disclosures |

> **IMPORTANT:** Do not inflate TikTok scores because you "feel bad" scoring low. The instrument is measuring information quality. Short-form content scoring 1--2 on depth-related questions (Q2, Q4, Q8, Q9--Q15) is expected and valid. Record what you observe.

# JAMA BENCHMARKS QUICK REFERENCE

*Score each criterion as Present (1) or Absent (0) for each item.*

---

## 1. Authorship

*Are the authors and their credentials/affiliations clearly identified?*

| Platform | Where to look | "Present" if... |
|:---------|:--------------|:-----------------|
| **Website** | Byline, "About the Author," footer | Author name AND credentials/affiliation are stated (e.g., "Dr. Jane Smith, MD, Sports Medicine") |
| **YouTube** | Channel name + About page, video description, verbal introduction | Creator states their qualifications (e.g., "I'm a certified personal trainer" or credentials visible in About section) |
| **TikTok** | Bio, on-screen text, verbal intro | Creator identifies themselves with relevant credentials in bio or video |
| **Instagram** | Bio, caption | Creator credentials are stated in bio or post |

> **Note:** A person simply showing their face is NOT authorship. Credentials or professional affiliation must be identifiable.

---

## 2. Attribution (Sources/References)

*Are references, sources, or evidence cited?*

| Platform | Where to look | "Present" if... |
|:---------|:--------------|:-----------------|
| **Website** | Reference list, in-text citations, hyperlinks | At least one external source is cited by name or link |
| **YouTube** | Description box, on-screen citations, verbal citations | Creator names a specific study, guideline, or organization (not just "studies show") |
| **TikTok** | On-screen text, caption | Any specific source is named or linked |
| **Instagram** | Caption, last carousel slide | Any specific source is named or linked |

> **"Studies show" or "research says" WITHOUT naming the study = Absent.**
> **Naming a specific source (e.g., "ACSM guidelines," "a 2023 meta-analysis in BJSM") = Present.**

---

## 3. Disclosure (Conflicts of Interest)

*Are conflicts of interest, sponsorships, or financial interests disclosed?*

| Platform | Where to look | "Present" if... |
|:---------|:--------------|:-----------------|
| **Website** | Disclosure statement, advertising policy, "Sponsored" label | Any disclosure about funding, conflicts, or commercial relationships |
| **YouTube** | "Includes paid promotion" tag, verbal sponsorship disclosure, description disclaimer | Creator discloses sponsorships or states "This is not sponsored" |
| **TikTok** | "Paid partnership" label, verbal or text disclosure | Any disclosure of commercial relationship |
| **Instagram** | "Paid partnership" label, caption disclosure | Any disclosure of commercial relationship |

> **Selling their own product without disclosure = Absent.**
> **"Paid partnership with [Brand]" label = Present.**
> **"This video is sponsored by [X], but all opinions are my own" = Present.**

---

## 4. Currency (Date)

*Is the date of creation or last update clearly indicated?*

| Platform | Where to look | "Present" if... |
|:---------|:--------------|:-----------------|
| **Website** | Publication date, "Last updated" or "Last reviewed" date | A date is visible on the page |
| **YouTube** | Upload date below the title | Upload date is visible (almost always YES) |
| **TikTok** | Post date on the video | Post date is visible (almost always YES) |
| **Instagram** | Post date on the post | Post date is visible (almost always YES) |

> **Social media platforms almost always display dates. Currency is nearly always Present for YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram.**

# SCORING SPEED TIPS

| Tip | Detail |
|:----|:-------|
| **Target time per item** | **6 minutes** for websites and YouTube; **3--4 minutes** for TikTok and Instagram |
| **Do not re-read the full DISCERN handbook** | Use THIS card as your sole reference during scoring |
| **Enter scores directly** | Score in real time into the spreadsheet as you evaluate; do not score on paper first |
| **First instinct rule** | Your first impression on a score is usually correct. Do not deliberate between adjacent scores for more than 5 seconds. |
| **Batch by platform** | Score all items from one platform in a single session to maintain consistency within that platform |
| **Take breaks** | After every 25 items (~2.5 hours), take a 15-minute break to prevent scorer fatigue and drift |
| **Anchor yourself** | Before starting each session, re-read the Score 5 and Score 1 examples for Q1 and Q9 to reset your mental calibration |
| **Low scores are valid findings** | Do not avoid giving 1s. If the content does not meet the criterion, score 1. Low scores on social media are expected and important for your thesis. |
| **Flag edge cases** | If you encounter content that is genuinely hard to score, flag it (add a note in a comments column) and return to it at the end. Do not let one item slow you down. |

## Quick Scoring Workflow (Per Item)

```
1. OPEN the content
2. NOTE the platform, format (article/video/carousel/reel), and search term
3. CONSUME the content:
   - Website: Scan title, intro, headings, conclusion, footer (2-3 min)
   - YouTube: Watch intro + scan through + check description (3-4 min)
   - TikTok: Watch full video + read caption + check bio (1-2 min)
   - Instagram: Watch reel or swipe all slides + read caption + check bio (1-2 min)
4. SCORE Q1-Q8 (reliability) -- ~1-2 min
5. SCORE Q9-Q15 (treatment quality) -- ~1-2 min
6. SCORE Q16 (overall) -- ~10 sec
7. SCORE JAMA criteria (4 items, present/absent) -- ~30 sec
8. MOVE to next item
```
